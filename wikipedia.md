# About

Wikipedia reading sessions.  

The dates indicate when I finished reading those articles, **NOT** when I started reading them. If you're curious to see what an article looked like during the date listed here, you can check on an article's edit history and select the revision that at least, is the closest to that date. 

Articles listed below are subject to change in the future, and may become redirects [(example)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Foxes), dead pages [(example)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deccan-Vijayanagar_Wars) or having entirely different content from when I read them at that date.

The articles listed below vary in length, ranging from [very short](https://w.wiki/CYyn) to [very long](https://w.wiki/3jb8) ones. Some of these may contain violent/offensive content, so yeah, have fun with that. Not my fault if an article contains blatant profanity in the title, but the unwavering decision of why did I even read the article in the first place, is uhh, because I can.

This list does not contain all of the Wikipedia articles I've ever read, but only those that I knew when I started recording this. I'm fairly certain that I remember reading the entirety of the article for [World War II](https://w.wiki/3M2t), I just don't remember when.

Some of you may argue that curating all these shit into a list with dates is a stupid idea, and you're right, it is a stupid idea. Almost seems like that I did this to prove a moot point that nobody asked.

Yeah, yeah, I've perpetually heard the ol' hackneyed adage that Wikipedia is an unreliable cesspool of horseshit that needs no introduction and therefore should be constantly avoided like the plague at all times. Should a statement as absurd as that would stop me from reading it is none of my concern. But really, you'd have to be seriously in a league lower than fucking stupid to think that referencing a Wikipedia article in your work as a student (e.g. a research paper) is a good idea.

I consider an article to be completely read if I know I read the very first word of an article, to the very last entry in its See Also or Notes section (if applicable). Anything beyond that is ignored.

You can use this [tool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User:Caorongjin/wordcount) to see how many words are in an [article](https://w.wiki/$F).

Note to self: you're absolutely allowed to read an article more than once.
